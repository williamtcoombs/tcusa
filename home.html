5a. Positions (classical)

Positions are conjugate pairs where, with the exception of the symmetric class (e.g., standing disengaged, kneeling, 50/50), one is superior in terms of offensive and defensive potential. We have arbitarily partitioned positions into two general categories (labelled "classical" and "leg-centric") to aid conceptualization, and also to buy us some time to deliberate the relative position metrics. As a general philosophy, seek to improve your position, looking for submission opportunities as they arise.

We provide a relative position metric, where a positive score denotes the superior position; whereas, a negative score denotes the inferior. For example, mount(+8) is the top/dominant/preferred position and mount(-8) is bottom/inferior/down/below/in trouble. A position score is best thought of as a range of scores, where realignment might change the advantage/disadvantage proportion in say the "50/50" to 60/40 or 70/30 depending on who has better control of the knee and access to a dangling ankle. Position ratings represent ordinal-scale approximations of relative desirability. The utility of a transition may be computed by subtracting the starting from the ending position, so, moving from bottom mount to bottom half guard is a 6-point [i.e., (-2)-(-8) = 6] improvement in position. This is conceptual guidance, not the scoring system used in IBJJF tournaments. It would be misleading to interpret these metrics too literally, as the metrics differ for each practitioner. If, for example, you are a Darce choke aficionado (e.g., Porter), then those positions where there is easier access (e.g., Side Control, Turtle) could be bumped up a little, whereas if you are weak in a position, let's arbitrarily choose guard, then your guard score should be reduced.

It is important to recognize that submissions are really positions in and of themselves. That is, a submission is simply a realignment within that position. Consider the triangle position, where the triangle choke and armbar are both viable options, or the omoplata, where the shoulder and wrist lock are readily available. Note also, advantage/disadvantage metric is rule dependent. For example, John Danaher considers the closed guard position to be neutral (0.0), which is a rational argument in the context of a Brazilian jiu-jitsu tournament, where striking is forbidden, whereas in a mixed martial arts competition where striking is permissible, the striking advantage tilts the scales in favor of the (upper body) top individual. The complexity increases swiftly as weapons, terrain, and teams are considered.
