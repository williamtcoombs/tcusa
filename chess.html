0. Blueprint

Spend sixty percent of your time on endgame and middle game, leaving forty percent for openings. Focus on a few openings that are inherently sound structurally, are not as dependent on move order, and where there is less memorization required. As White, consider the London System. As Black, pick a response to 1.e4 (e.g., Petrov Defense, Caro-Kann Defense, French Defense, Pirc) and something for 1.d4 (e.g., Slav Defense, King's Indian Defense).

4. Opening Principles

You advantage going into the middle game will primarily be the result of your optimization of opening principles, including: (a) the principle of harmony, (b) the principle of the center, (c) king safety, (d) the principle of development, (e) the principle of time, (f) taking the initiative, and (g) weakening the opponent's king defense.

[319] opening principles: Hercules Chess

Principle of Harmony

Use all your pieces.

Don’t block your pieces.

Don’t block your center pawns.


Principle of the Center

Keep a pawn in the center.

Centralize your pieces.


King Safety

Castle.

Don’t move the pawns in front of the castled king.


Principle of Development

Develop your pieces.

Give your pieces freedom.

Don’t move too many pawns.


Principle of Time

Use every move to improve your position.

Develop with tempo.

Don’t move the same piece twice.


Taking the Initiative

Develop with threats.


Weaken the Opponent's Defense of Their King.

Prevent castling.

Open attacking lines (e.g., files, diagonals).

Wreck their pawn structure.

Drive their king out into the open.

Eliminate defending pieces.

10. Flank Openings

A flank opening is typified by play on one or both flanks, the portion of the chess board outside the central d and e files.


A. The English Opening

1.c4 is the fourth most popular opening and is considered reliable and flexible. White begins the fight for the centre by staking a claim to the d5-square from the wing, in hypermodern style. This opening is often used as a transpositional device in much the same way as 1.Nf3 - to avoid such highly regarded responses to 1.d4 as the Nimzo-Indian and Grünfeld Defense.

17. Academic Findings

Researchers found: (a) a strong correlation between chess skill and number of hours of practice; (b) group practice was a better predictor of high-level performance than individual practice; (c) masters had a higher chess rating than expert players after only three years of serious dedication to chess, although there were no differences in the number of hours of practice. The difference that may explain the variation in rating is that masters start practising at an earlier age than experts. (d) Activities such as reading books and using computer software (game databases, but not playing programs) were important for the development of high-level performance.
