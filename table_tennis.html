2. The Grip

We will focus on the shakehands grip, but the penhold grip certainly has some advantages worth considering. Ben Larcombe provides insight into grip nuance based upon several months of experimentation, especially how the label shakehands is intuitively potentially misleading because it diverts attention to the lower fingers when perhaps the focus should be on the pinching action between the thumb and forefinger, what he calls "the relaxed shakehands grip." The Shakehand Grip Technique There are variations (e.g., traditional, deep, shallow) of the shakehand grip from which to choose as well as other viable penhold grip alternatives (e.g., Chinese, Japanese, Korean). Less common options also exist (e.g., reverse backhand grip, Seemiller grip).

21. Academic Findings

Although table tennis has a tradition lasting more than 100 years, relatively little is known about players' physiological requirements - especially during competition. In this review we discuss research studies that have led to our current understanding of how the body functions during table tennis training and competition and how this is altered by training. Match and practice analysis of the table tennis game indicates that during intense practice and competition it is predominantly the anaerobic alactic system that is called into play, while the endurance system is relied on to recovery the anaerobic stores used during such effort. It is thus important for coaches to keep in mind that, while the anaerobic alactic system is the most energetic system used during periods of exertion in a table tennis game, a strong capacity for endurance is what helps a player recover quicker for the following match and the next day of competition.
