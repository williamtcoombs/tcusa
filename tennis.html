0. Blueprint

Initially, focus on technical precision [e.g., grips, footwork, checkpoints (body, racquet), energy transferal (foot, body, arm, racquet, ball), racquet (angle, path, velocity), and ball path (height, depth, direction)] as you work to improve your strokes. Control is key, as even a child can blast the ball over the fence. Once you can hit the ball where you dictate, add pace; then, master strategy.

Roy Barth [191] advises: (a) stay low, (b) hit and move (i.e., don't just stand and watch), (c) finish with your hand in the direction of the target, (d) keep a relaxed grip during racquet preparation, (e) line up the oncoming ball with the butt of the racquet on volleys and groundstrokes, (f) freeze your eyes and head at the point of impact on every stroke, (g) on serves and overheads, rotate sideways in relation to the target, relax your grip, have your arm up, spring up, and keep your eyes up, (h) with regard to racquet head speed, swing slow to faster on groundstrokes and your serve, (i) use hand leverage as the key for power and control, and (j) take shorter steps the closer you are to the ball.

Of these, freezing your eyes and head at the point of contact is the most important, as this helps you to hit the middle of the strings, keep your follow through in the line of your target, and makes your shot more deceptive because you don't telegraph your intention with the movement of your head.

Some critical shots include: (a) slice serve, (b) topspin forehand, (c) slice backhand, (d) defensive lob, (e) approach shot, (f) moonball, (g) short angle volleys, (h) crosscourt backhand, (i) inside-out forehand, and (j) overhead.

Tom Sadzeck [370] suggests that you craft your own personal tennis program, to include: development of fundamental stroke technique, strategy, isolation of common situations and patterns, footwork drills, understanding opponents' strengths and weaknesses, mental training under pressure conditions, and maintaining stimulation through hours of repetition during practice activities.

Devoting the rest of your life to improving, and then slowing the process of decay, of one of the world's greatest sports, is a life well spent.

19. Doubles Communication

Our friends John and Jeri remind each other at the start and any time nervousness is apparent: "Breathe. Watch the ball. Move your feet."

We use two-part signals for tactics. For serve location: (a) "1" denotes serving to the "T"; (b) "2" denotes a body serve; (c) "3" denotes serving wide. We use a second signal for poaching intent: (a) a closed fist denotes staying put and (b) five fingers denotes "I am poaching!" We also fake poach once per game, left to the discretion of the net player.

Mistakes are part of every match. How you respond may help (or hinder). It is easy to encourage when your partner makes a good shot (e.g., "Awesome!"). The challenge is when they (or you) make mistakes. We advocate having the player that made the mistake take the initiative, saying nothing or periodically self-identifying the error, perhaps using an expression such as "Remind me to ____": (a) "...watch the ball." (b) "...move my feet." (c)"...shorten my backswing." (d) "... call the ball." (e) "... attack the center of the court." (f) "... target the opponent's feet." (g) "... attack the net." (h) "... hit deep." (i) "... target the net player's backhand." (j) "... windshield at the net." (k) "... lob." (l) "... aim at the middle net strap." (m) "... pinch." (n) "... avoid no (wo)man's land." (o) "... communicate."

24. What is tiebreaker (aka tiebreak aka tie-break)?

Like so many things, it depends. Which league? Which tournament? What is the game count in the set? Is the match singles, doubles, or mixed doubles? Is it the final set of the match? It is further confusing because of naming conventions, as for example, a 10-point tiebreaker is the first to ten points (win by two), a logical convention, whereas a 12-point tiebreaker is the first to seven points (win by two), a non-intuitive convention.

7-point tiebreaker (aka 12-point tiebreaker)

First to seven, win by two (e.g., 7-0, 0-7, 7-5, 8-6, 218-216) points. As the total game count in the set is even, there is no change of court. The first point is served to the deuce court by the player who received in the previous game. After the first point, players switch courts. From this point on, each server serves two points, the first from the add court, the second from the deuce courts. Players again switch courts after six total games or multiples of six games. After the tiebreaker is completed, the set game count will be odd, leading to another change of courts if the match is not yet concluded.


10-point tiebreaker (aka super tiebreaker)

First to ten, win by two (e.g., 0-10, 10-8, 11-9) points. First serve is to add court by player (or team) A. Then, player B serves twice, first from the deuce court and then from the add court. This two-serve pattern continues until there is a winner.


Coman tiebreak

Players again switch courts after the first game and then after four total games or multiples of four games, potentially reducing an advantage due to the elements (e.g., wind, sun).


Advantage set

No tiebreaker here; it is the first to six games in a set, win by two (e.g., 6-4, 7-5) games.


Wimbledon

If the final set is tied at 6-6, the seventh game is decided with a 10-point tiebreaker.


French Open

7-point tiebreakers are used for all but the deciding set, which uses a 10-point tiebreaker.


Australian Open

7-point tiebreakers are used for all but the deciding set, which uses a 10-point tiebreaker. Mixed doubles uses a 10-point tiebreaker in lieu of a third set if there is a tie after two sets.


US Open

Women's singles, and women's and men's doubles matches are a best-of-3-sets format; men's singles a best-of-five-sets. If any set is tied at 6-6, the seventh game is decided with a 7-point tiebreaker, with the exception of the final set, which uses a 10-point tiebreaker. For mixed doubles, a 10-point tiebreaker is used instead of a third set.


United States Tennis Association (USTA)

If a set is tied 6-6, the seventh game is decided by a 7-point tiebreaker.
