15. Doubles Communication

Our friends John and Jeri remind each other at the start and any time nervousness is apparent: "Breathe. Watch the ball. Move your feet."

We use two-part signals for tactics. For serve location: (a) "1" denotes serving to the "T"; (b) "2" denotes a body serve; (c) "3" denotes serving wide. We use a second signal for poaching intent: (a) a closed fist denotes staying put and (b) five fingers denotes "I am poaching!" We also fake poach once per game, left to the discretion of the net player.

Mistakes are part of every match. How you respond may help (or hinder). It is easy to encourage when your partner makes a good shot (e.g., "Awesome!"). The challenge is when they (or you) make mistakes. We advocate having the player that made the mistake take the initiative, saying nothing or periodically self-identifying the error, perhaps using an expression such as "Remind me to ____": (a) "...watch the ball." (b) "...move my feet." (c)"...shorten my backswing." (d) "... call the ball." (e) "... attack the center of the court." (f) "... target the opponent's feet." (g) "... attack the net." (h) "... hit deep." (i) "... target the net player's backhand." (j) "... windshield at the net." (k) "... lob." (l) "... aim at the middle net strap." (m) "... pinch." (n) "... avoid no (wo)man's land." (o) "... communicate."

20. What is tiebreaker (aka tiebreak aka tie-break)?

Like so many things, it depends. Which league? Which tournament? What is the game count in the set? Is the match singles, doubles, or mixed doubles? Is it the final set of the match? It is further confusing because of naming conventions, as for example, a 10-point tiebreaker is the first to ten points (win by two), a logical convention, whereas a 12-point tiebreaker is the first to seven points (win by two), a non-intuitive convention.

[169] Isner vs Opelka: Tennis TV
[113] Wikipedia
[114] Master Class
[115] USTA

7-point tiebreaker (aka 12-point tiebreaker)

First to seven, win by two (e.g., 7-0, 0-7, 7-5, 8-6, 218-216) points. As the total game count in the set is even, there is no change of court. The first point is served to the deuce court by the player who received in the previous game. After the first point, players switch courts. From this point on, each server serves two points, the first from the add court, the second from the deuce courts. Players again switch courts after six total games or multiples of six games. After the tiebreaker is completed, the set game count will be odd, leading to another change of courts if the match is not yet concluded.


10-point tiebreaker (aka super tiebreaker)

First to ten, win by two (e.g., 0-10, 10-8, 11-9) points. First serve is to add court by player (or team) A. Then, player B serves twice, first from the deuce court and then from the add court. This two-serve pattern continues until there is a winner.


Coman tiebreak

Players again switch courts after the first game and then after four total games or multiples of four games, potentially reducing an advantage due to the elements (e.g., wind, sun).


Advantage set

No tiebreaker here; it is the first to six games in a set, win by two (e.g., 6-4, 7-5) games.


Wimbledon

If the final set is tied at 12-12, the seventh game is decided with a 10-point tiebreaker.


French Open

12-point tiebreakers are used for all but the deciding set. In the final set, an advantage set decides the outcome.


Australian Open

If tied 6-6 in the final set of a match, a 10-point tiebreaker is used. Earlier sets tied at 6-6 use a 7-point tiebreaker. Mixed doubles uses a 10-point tiebreaker in lieu of a third set if there is a tie after two sets.


US Open

Women's singles, and women's and men's doubles matches are a best-of-3-sets format; men's singles a best-of-five-sets. If any set is tied at 6-6, the seventh game is decided with a 12-point tiebreaker. For mixed doubles, a 12-point tiebreaker is used instead of a third set.


United States Tennis Association (USTA)

If a set is tied 6-6, the seventh game is decided by a 12-point tiebreaker.
